WGU — How It Actually Works
Competency Units (CU) vs. GPA WGU does not use GPA. There are no letter grades. You either demonstrate competency and pass, or you continue working until you do. Each course is assigned a number of Competency Units — roughly analogous to credit hours at traditional schools. You need 120 CU total to graduate. 79 of those are already satisfied by prior credentials (A.S. degree, CompTIA A+, Per Scholas bootcamp). The remaining 41 CU are the active curriculum on this calendar.
Minimum requirement per term Each term is six months and has a flat tuition cost regardless of how many courses you complete. You must complete at least one course unit per term to remain enrolled. There is no maximum — students with prior background routinely complete a full term's worth of courses in 6–10 weeks and use the remaining time for depth work. Acceleration is financially rewarded: finishing fast means paying for fewer terms.
Proctored exams — what students don't expect Most WGU assessments are performance tasks (written projects graded by evaluators). Some courses have proctored objective assessments — timed multiple-choice exams taken through a browser-based proctoring system. These require: a quiet private room, a webcam pointed at your face and workspace, and a government ID. A separate external webcam is strongly recommended — built-in laptop cameras are often too narrow to show the required workspace view and can trigger flags. Test this setup before your first proctored exam, not on the day.
Common misconceptions Online does not mean open book for proctored exams. The proctoring software monitors your screen, webcam feed, and microphone for the duration of the exam. Performance tasks are open-resource but evaluated on demonstrated understanding — a submitted task that reads as AI-generated or copied will fail. The competency model means you can retake assessments, but there are cooldown periods. Passing on the first attempt is worth optimizing for.
NeetCode — Why It's in This Calendar
What NeetCode is NeetCode (neetcode.io) is a structured interview prep resource built around a curated problem set — the NeetCode 150 — organized by pattern rather than by difficulty. Where LeetCode is a database of 2500+ problems, NeetCode is an opinionated subset with video explanations focused on the 15 core patterns that cover the vast majority of SWE interview questions. For students earlier in their CS journey, the pattern framing is more useful than grinding random problems.
The 15 core patterns Arrays & Hashing · Two Pointers · Sliding Window · Stack · Binary Search · Linked List · Trees · Tries · Heap / Priority Queue · Backtracking · Graphs · Advanced Graphs · 1D Dynamic Programming · 2D Dynamic Programming · Bit Manipulation. This calendar introduces patterns in the order they appear in the NeetCode roadmap, synchronized with the LeetCode problems for that week.
How to use it alongside LeetCode Watch the NeetCode video explanation for a problem before attempting it independently if you're stuck — not as a substitute for solving it yourself. The goal is pattern recognition, not memorization. When you can solve a new problem in a pattern you've seen before without looking up the approach, the pattern has landed. That's the signal to move on.
NeetCode roadmap vs. NeetCode 150 The roadmap (free at neetcode.io/roadmap) shows all 150 problems organized by pattern with difficulty ratings. Start here. The NeetCode 150 is the full problem set filterable on LeetCode directly. For Phases 1–2 of this calendar, you're working through Arrays & Hashing, Two Pointers, and Sliding Window — roughly the first 30 problems on the roadmap. Do not skip ahead. Pattern depth beats problem count at this stage.
