Exploratory Phase

Software development is easy to approach abstractly. Syntax can be learned from documentation, concepts from textbooks, and architecture from diagrams. What those sources rarely convey is systems context — how code behaves at runtime, what performance sequencing means, or why architectural decisions matter in practice.

I had prior exposure to HTML and CSS years earlier, but that abstraction gap remained. Analysis paralysis, perfectionism, and an over-emphasis on theory slowed progress early on. Rewriting notes is not the same as building. Watching tutorials is not the same as debugging.

The turning point came when learning moved into real development environments — version control, deployment realities, broken builds, unclear requirements. The question shifted from “Can I learn syntax?” to “What is the job actually?”

That shift in perspective was the catalyst for everything that followed.

Theory and Practice

The bibliography below includes both required texts and deeper computer science material. I have not completed every optional theory text — that is intentional.

My priority has been applied systems fluency: frameworks, databases, deployment, distributed systems concepts, and reliability. Theory is studied where it clarifies performance, limits, and architectural trade-offs.

Teaching reshaped this sequencing.

Students from CS, cybersecurity, and mathematics backgrounds who understood computation models, memory, and architecture at a mechanical level advanced quickly in applied contexts — even when they had written less application code initially.

Conversely, strong coders who could scaffold websites quickly often became blocked when systems failed — unclear about what the runtime was doing, how memory was being used, or why sequencing mattered.

The pattern held consistently enough to reshape this curriculum: start from the machine, not the framework. Understand what the runtime is doing before optimizing for developer experience.

My students were never incapable of abstraction. They lacked context. Once grounded in systems thinking, they learned faster — and with more confidence.

Teaching others forced me to refine my own mental models. In that process, I began teaching myself more effectively.

Teaching as Reinforcement

I tutor students in software engineering and computer science while drawing insights from cybersecurity and game development communities — particularly in security, performance, and robust C++ design patterns.

My applied mathematics explorations (including Unity-based visualization projects) exist primarily to prevent conceptual atrophy — both for myself and my students. Abstract ideas retain meaning when manipulated directly.

Students who once struggled to write a simple loop have gone on to build full programs. That growth reshaped my understanding of what consistent systems thinking can do.

For non-technical professionals curious about programming, this curriculum is also an invitation. Systems literacy is not reserved for those pursuing formal computer science degrees.

Operations and Applied Tooling

My interests increasingly lean toward operational leverage — building internal tools that reduce unnecessary friction and free time for deeper learning and higher-impact work.

The current scripting challenge (see 2026-03-scripting-challenge.html) reflects this transition: developing instinctive fluency in Python to implement ideas more quickly rather than over-planning them.

Planning once consumed more time than execution. The goal now is balanced iteration — build, measure, refine.

AI as Instrumentation

I experiment with local and hosted models (DeepSeek, Mistral/Mixtral, Llama 3 via Ollama) to compare reasoning patterns, hallucination tendencies, and contextual limits.

AI functions as instrumentation — generating edge cases, stress-testing understanding, and accelerating iteration — not as a replacement for systems knowledge.

Discernment determines return on investment. There are many tools available; judgment determines which ones compound. That includes knowing which uses of AI are worth resisting entirely.

In February 2026, Anthropic lost a $200 million Pentagon contract rather than remove two restrictions from its usage policy: no use of Claude for mass domestic surveillance of Americans, and no use in fully autonomous weapons systems. The Trump administration responded by directing every federal agency to cease use of Anthropic's technology and designating the company a supply chain risk — a designation previously reserved for foreign adversaries. Anthropic's public position: current frontier models are not reliable enough for autonomous weapons, and mass surveillance of citizens is a violation of fundamental rights regardless of whether current law has caught up to that reality. The contract terms were known to the Pentagon when it signed in July 2025. The restrictions had never blocked a single military mission. The dispute was about who gets to set the boundary — not whether the boundary was causing harm. That distinction matters.

Security-First Mindset

A recurring finding across developer surveys: roughly 85% of engineers do not actively account for known vulnerability classes during development. Security gets treated as a post-launch concern, a separate team's problem, or an afterthought patched in before release. The attack surface doesn't wait for the launch date.

I build with the attack surface in mind from the start — auth flows, input handling, dependency choices, and supply chain exposure considered before implementation begins. This is the same reasoning behind writing a salted PBKDF2 hash function before touching the login form, or reading CVE disclosures the way other people read release notes.

Two incidents in particular shaped how I think about this. The Next.js middleware auth bypass (CVE-2025-29927) demonstrated how a framework-level trust assumption — affecting a significant share of production web infrastructure — can be exploited silently across thousands of applications before a patch exists. The XZ Utils supply chain attack (2024) is the deeper case: a compression library embedded throughout Linux infrastructure, nearly compromised by a contributor who spent two years building trust in the open source community before inserting a backdoor into the build system. It was caught almost by accident. The systems at risk included government, financial, and healthcare infrastructure. The attack surface was not a misconfigured server — it was patient human trust, exploited methodically over time.

These are not exotic edge cases. They are the shape of how serious compromises actually happen. Staying current on them is part of the work.

A dedicated post on building with a security-first mindset — including practical exposure through Python-based security testing — is in progress. See the log.

Professional Foundation

Prior to entering technology full-time, I built expertise in specialty coffee — studying processing methods, varietals, roasting science, extraction theory, and sensory evaluation under Specialty Coffee Association guidelines.

I placed second nationally in a specialty coffee equipment manufacturer's competition and led workshops at the brand's flagship NYC retail location — calibrating technical depth without overwhelming a general audience.

Teaching extraction theory, weighted flow control, and regional tasting characteristics refined my public speaking and audience calibration skills — gauging how technical to be without overwhelming.

That period ended on a professional high note and marked the point at which independence and discipline became transferable assets. Systems thinking did not begin in software — it matured there.

What follows is the 30-month plan. The courses are real WGU courses. The books are the ones that actually matter for the work. The projects are built around things that broke in the real world and needed fixing.
